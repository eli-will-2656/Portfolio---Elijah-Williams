Salvador Dali

About

Salvador Dali is one of the most well-known artists of the 20th century, but also perhaps one of the least understood. Dali had a long and controversial career -- from his highly influential Surrealist paintings, to his experiments with Cubism completed during his youth -- his paintings are often well-recognized.

No other major 20th century artist combines such widespread popular appeal with so much critical disdain from official institutions and historicans of modern art. Is is important to know the depth and variety of Dali's expressions: Dali was not only a painter, but also a writer poet, theorist, print-maker, photographer, scultor, theater designer... you get the message. Although a man of multifarious production, what Dali emphasized the most in his commentaries and philsophy was painting.

Dali was both an international celebrity and an intensely local artist, rooted in Catalan culture and landscape. Trilingual in Catalan, French, and Spanish he made French his operational language, allowing him to a play a central role in international surrealism.

Dali knew he wanted to be a painter since he was a young child painting rocks at his mother's vacation house allowing the shores of Spain, but he lived through an age increasingly critical and despising of painting. These were a few of the many paradoxes that Dali both faced and embraced during his life -- photography, film, installations, conceptual art, Dada's objects, all challenged pictorial representation. Dali both bigorously contribued to these alternative modes of artistic practice and continued to maintain the validity of painting.
